Love as the medium

Are you looking for an easy way to connect with more people on your next website or app? Let me introduce some key concepts. I believe love, gratitude, and appreciation are safe spaces where the next generation of software will move in the coming years. As social software creators, this comes with sacrifices when designing experiences that can also make your own creation grow. It‚Äôs a tough puzzle. Intention vs IncentiveThe biggest software platforms in our world have implemented techniques to keep users spending more time on their apps, which contradicts their intended purpose. Platforms like Instagram, YouTube, and TikTok also include screen time reminders and usage dashboards that allow users to monitor or limit how long they stay. They also have access to professionals and psychologists on their teams who help shape the design itself. That‚Äôs the intention.

Now from intention to incentive. This is like fame, but even harder. In some ways, getting attention can be easy. But getting it right, and ethically, is not. The incentive to grow often conflicts with the incentive to create meaning.

Growth rewards attention. Attention rewards engagement. Engagement often rewards what is stimulating, polarizing, addictive, or emotionally intense. Not necessarily what is meaningful.

This is where the conflict begins.

Intention says help people live better lives.

Incentive says keep them here longer.

Building useful tools is relatively easy. If something simplifies your life automagically you adopt it. That part is straightforward. The only difficulty being how to present it to the user in the simplest way possible

Social software is different. It doesn‚Äôt solve a problem itself. There is a very strong ‚ÄúWhy?‚Äù. Because it shapes behavior. It amplifies our human desires. It changes how people see themselves and others.

The real challenge is not technical. It is cultural and psychological. You cannot fully explain why a social product works before it works. It is like explaining a joke. Once you explain it, it loses its magic.

Even teams with product managers, analysts, and psychologists struggle with this. Technology is no longer the hard part. Distribution is. Alignment is. Designing incentives that do not betray the original intention is.

In the end, incentives shape behavior more than intention ever will.

Incentives Shape BehaviorTwitter offers a clear example of how incentives shape behavior.

At different points, its algorithm appeared to amplify disagreement. People are more likely to reply when they disagree than when they agree. Disagreement drives engagement. Politics becomes fuel. If your only goal is growth, this makes sense. But what grows is not always what improves the experience.

When outrage becomes the engine, the feed degrades. The product may grow in numbers while declining in quality. Some of these changes were later adjusted or reverted. It is unclear for me whether that was due to public backlash or measurable drops in activity. What is clear is that engagement alone is not a reliable proxy for value.

To build a good social product, you need rules. And you also need growth. At first glance, this seems opposed to pure free speech. But growth does not require chaos. Structure does not mean suppression. Structure means designing boundaries that protect the experience.

There was a theory that if Nikita Bier had joined Twitter as Head of Product, he might have influenced a different direction. Not because free speech is inherently wrong, but because unlimited speech is not always useful for a product. Nikita has argued that limitations are sometimes necessary to prevent the worst actors from dominating a network. Without constraints, the loudest voices often crowd out everyone else.

The counterargument is that Twitter is meant to be an open space. That is the ideal. But it is also the hardest version to maintain. If your product attempts to operate at the full extent of legal freedom, you inherit the complexity, the conflict, and the cultural fragmentation that come with it. You are free to do it. But the incentives become harder to manage.

At massive scale, changing direction is extremely difficult. The larger the network, the more locked-in its behaviors become.

Creators like you should not sacrifice the long-term goal of their software for values they do not truly believe in. Loving the medium is not the same as loving the outcome. If the incentives and the values are misaligned, the product eventually reflects the incentives.

And in the long run, misalignment is unsustainable.

Constraint Creates MeaningThe quality of the compliments users sent on Gas was astonishing. When you see that, it becomes difficult to argue for total openness. It worked because it had boundaries. Users could not write anything they wanted. The structure shaped the tone. Without that structure, you already know what would have happened.

Sometimes saying no creates more meaning than saying yes.

Constraints are not always limitations. Sometimes they are the source of magic. In the physical world, open space feels liberating. On the internet, too much openness can feel empty or chaotic. A well-designed restriction can focus attention and elevate quality.

Dating apps offer a useful contrast.

The standard dynamic is simple. Create a profile. Swipe. Wait for matches. Much of the revenue depends on anticipation. ‚ÄúWho liked me? I need to find out.‚Äù The excitement lives in the discovery. The moment you find someone meaningful, the app often disappears from your phone. Hinge captures this tension in its slogan, ‚ÄúThe dating app designed to be deleted.‚Äù In theory, your success is their success.

Now how do you as a software creator can align your purpose with your user‚Äôs purpose?

Sacrifice is the key. There is a new trend that clarifies this intention by blocking all conversations that the user may have if they have a match. Let‚Äôs say you got two matches, A and B. You can only talk to A unless you are certain you don‚Äôt like A, then you can talk with B and go to the next one. One to one. You could have multiple matches, but never more than one chat. That preserves attention only for that person. They sacrificed optionality, something so searched for in dating, in order to create meaning. They sacrificed engagement because they reduced the number of human actors connected, which reduces notifications and therefore reduces the time you spend in the app. But it was worth it. If this is the first time you‚Äôre reading this, it seems like the best idea in the world.

Designing for EmotionAnother human emotion that is rarely discussed in product design, yet is increasingly relevant, is music.

People want to share their favorite songs, defend their underrated tracks, debate overrated ones, and find others who feel the same way. Unlike many other social signals, music carries emotional depth. And today, integrating music into software is no longer technically complex. APIs exist. Data is accessible. The barrier is not infrastructure. It is imagination.

Music also generates rich behavioral data. People who listen to niche genres behave differently from those who follow mainstream trends. People who explore everything are rarer than we assume. Taste is not random. It signals personality, rhythm, mood, and even social patterns.

From a growth perspective, the mechanics are almost obvious.

It has a feature similar to Bumble for Friends, another like Threads, a global chat feature (not bad at all), and another for taking quizzes about your favorite artist.

This is the key point in design: what things do you need to remove or restrict that could ‚Äòharm‚Äô you as a creator? Whether it‚Äôs because they don‚Äôt generate much revenue, or because they don‚Äôt generate much growth (I think this hurts more in the long run), but which could be beneficial for your user.

Creator ResponsibilityIf it‚Äôs not easy to see, unlike real life, when people act inside our software, we are responsible for what happens there. We design the incentives. We design the constraints. We design what scales.

We live in a transparent world now. It‚Äôs harder to ignore when something feels off in a product. One of my core points is this. Practice empathy. Awareness is a good rule of thumb when building products for humans.

Sometimes the business model doesn‚Äôt work or doesn‚Äôt align with consumer trends, and that‚Äôs okay. It doesn‚Äôt always have to work. And it‚Äôs social software. Someone has probably tried it before. Above all, we know our chances of success are slim, and we already know that.

Activity is the key ingredient of a social space. Threads is successful, but it doesn‚Äôt have the same depth of connection as Twitter. Its user base comes largely from Instagram, and those users behave differently. Many expected Meta to dominate through acquisition alone. They didn‚Äôt.

For new social apps, the opening is rarely consistent effort. It‚Äôs closer to a miracle. Managing a universal experience is incredibly hard. Maybe the medium itself is the constraint, especially if the goal is to make people feel better.

Can we design systems where growth and meaning reinforce each other instead of competing? Because that might be the product problem of this decade.
